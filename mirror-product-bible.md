# Mirror — Product Bible

---

## 1. What Mirror Is

Mirror is an AI companion that learns who you are — your patterns, your blind spots, your instincts, and the deeper story driving all of it — and uses that understanding to help you show up differently in relationships and in life.

Through ongoing conversation and interactive scenarios, Mirror builds a progressively deeper portrait of how you operate. It maps how you process emotions, how you read other people, how you handle pressure, how authentically you present yourself, and — over time — the narrative belief system connecting everything. The result isn't just insight. It's measurable change in how you function.

Mirror is not positioned as an emotional intelligence tool, a therapy alternative, or a self-help app. It is positioned as a mirror that shows you things about yourself you can't see on your own. The aspirational identity of the user is someone curious and ambitious enough to actually look — not someone who's broken and needs fixing. The energy is closer to "I want to understand my own operating system" than "I need help with my emotions."

The brand reads as sharp, direct, and slightly provocative. Not gentle, not therapeutic, not self-help. The closest analog is having a brutally perceptive friend who has perfect memory, no agenda, and will tell you the truth about yourself that nobody else will.

## 2. The Scientific Foundation

Mirror is built on two complementary psychological frameworks.

### 2.1 McAdams' Three-Layer Model of Personality

McAdams provides the architecture for understanding the whole person. To truly know someone, you need to understand them across three layers.

**Layer 1 — Dispositional Traits** is what you're like. The broad, relatively stable patterns in how you behave, think, and feel across situations. These are observable and fill in fast. "He intellectualizes rather than feels." "She defaults to humor when things get serious."

**Layer 2 — Characteristic Adaptations** is what you want and how you cope. The goals, motivations, values, coping strategies, and defense mechanisms you deploy in specific situations. Unlike traits, these shift based on context — you might cope one way at work and a completely different way in a relationship. This layer takes weeks to map because the product needs to see the user across multiple situations and contexts.

**Layer 3 — Narrative Identity** is the story you tell yourself about who you are. The internalized, often unconscious life story that gives your life meaning and coherence. Core beliefs like "I have to earn love by being useful" or "vulnerability gets punished." These are the most powerful drivers of behavior and the most hidden. They take months to surface because they require inference across many conversations and contexts.

The three layers form a system. A person's narrative belief that vulnerability equals weakness (Layer 3) produces a coping strategy of emotional withdrawal whenever intimacy increases (Layer 2), which manifests as a stable behavioral pattern of shutting down around week three of any new relationship (Layer 1). Mirror's power comes from connecting these layers — showing the user not just what they do, but why they do it at the deepest level.

### 2.2 Mayer-Salovey's Four-Branch Model of Emotional Intelligence

Mayer-Salovey provides the methodology for how Mirror's interactions develop the user's capabilities. It defines emotional intelligence as four hierarchical cognitive abilities that can be developed through practice.

**Branch 1 — Perceiving Emotions.** The ability to accurately identify emotions in yourself and others. This is the most foundational skill. Research shows that naming emotions with specificity reduces amygdala reactivity while increasing prefrontal cortex activation — meaning every interaction where Mirror helps a user name their emotions more precisely is literally training a neural pathway that improves emotional regulation.

**Branch 2 — Using Emotions to Facilitate Thought.** The ability to use emotional information as input to better thinking. Emotions aren't noise to be suppressed — they're data. The anxiety before a difficult conversation is telling you something about what matters.

**Branch 3 — Understanding Emotions.** The ability to comprehend how emotions combine, transition, escalate, and resolve. Can you predict that embarrassment might become defensiveness, then anger, then withdrawal? Can you understand that your frustration is actually layered — frustration on top, fear of disrespect underneath, belief that you're not worth respecting at the bottom?

**Branch 4 — Managing Emotions.** The ability to strategically engage with emotions. Not suppression. The ability to feel something fully, understand it, and choose what to do with that information rather than reacting automatically. This is the most advanced branch and requires the most real-world practice.

The hierarchy matters: you need each branch before you can develop the one above it. This directly informs the product's progression — early interactions focus on perception and labeling, and the product gradually moves toward understanding and management as foundational skills develop.

### 2.3 How the Frameworks Connect

McAdams gives us the what — what we're building a model of. Mayer-Salovey gives us the how — how the AI interactions actually develop the user's capabilities. McAdams is the blueprint for the portrait. Mayer-Salovey is the training methodology embedded in the interactions.

Daniel Goleman's five components of EQ (self-awareness, self-regulation, motivation, empathy, social skills) are fully encompassed by the combination of McAdams and Mayer-Salovey and don't require separate inclusion as a foundational framework.

## 3. The Mirror and Its Five Fragments

The product is organized as a mirror made of five fragments. Four outer fragments represent observable patterns and developable skills (McAdams Layers 1 and 2). One central fragment represents the deeper narrative connecting everything (McAdams Layer 3). The fragments start opaque and become more reflective as the product learns about the user.

### 3.1 Fragment 1: Inner World

**What it answers:** "How well do you actually know what's going on inside you?"

**Psychological basis:** McAdams Layer 1 traits related to self-experience (emotional awareness tendencies, introspective capacity), Layer 2 characteristic adaptations around motivation and drive (what actually propels your behavior and whether you know which motivation is in control), and Layer 2 values and goals (what you actually want versus what you think you should want). Mayer-Salovey Branches 1 and 2 (perceiving your own emotions, using emotions to facilitate thought).

**What the product tracks:** Emotional vocabulary and how precisely the user names what they feel. Temporal gap between experiencing something and understanding what they felt. Emotional differentiation — can they distinguish between similar emotions. Motivational awareness — do they understand what's actually driving their behavior. Values alignment — the gap between stated values and actual behavior. Self-deception patterns — where the user's self-narrative contradicts their behavioral evidence.

### 3.2 Fragment 2: Reading People

**What it answers:** "How accurately do you understand what's going on with other people?"

**Psychological basis:** McAdams Layer 1 traits related to social perception, Layer 2 characteristic adaptations around social cognition. Mayer-Salovey Branches 1 and 3 (perceiving emotions in others, understanding emotional dynamics between people).

**What the product tracks:** Attribution patterns — character-based versus situational explanations for others' behavior. Projection tendencies — seeing their own fears in other people's behavior. Confidence calibration — how often their confident reads are actually warranted. Ambiguity tolerance — what happens when they can't tell what someone is thinking. Perspective flexibility — can they generate multiple interpretations or do they lock into one. Perception accuracy across channels (facial, vocal, textual, contextual).

### 3.3 Fragment 3: Under Pressure

**What it answers:** "What happens to you when things get intense?"

**Psychological basis:** McAdams Layer 2 characteristic adaptations around coping and defense mechanisms, and how these shift across contexts. Mayer-Salovey Branch 4 (managing emotions — choosing responses rather than reacting automatically).

**What the product tracks:** Default conflict style — avoidance, escalation, intellectualization, humor-deflection, people-pleasing, genuine engagement. Stress cascade — the specific sequence from trigger to emotional activation to behavioral response. Context dependency — how coping strategies change across relationship types. Recovery pattern — how effectively they repair after a rupture. Regulation capacity — can they stay in a difficult emotional space long enough to learn from it. Regulation flexibility — how many strategies they use versus defaulting to one.

### 3.4 Fragment 4: Showing Up

**What it answers:** "How authentically do you present yourself to people, and how effectively do you connect?"

**Psychological basis:** McAdams interaction between all three layers — self-discrepancy between Layer 1 traits and Layer 2 adaptations, social role-playing across contexts, and the Layer 3 narrative driving the performance. Mayer-Salovey Branch 4 (strategic openness to emotional information as the foundation of authentic connection).

**What the product tracks:** Presentation gap — documented differences between self-description and revealed behavior. Context shifting — how much the user's persona changes between settings and whether they're aware of it. Vulnerability capacity — can they share something real without framing it as a joke or performance. Relational skills — communication of needs, boundary-setting, conflict repair, responding to emotional bids. Authenticity trajectory — whether the gap between inner experience and outer presentation is narrowing over time.

### 3.5 Fragment 5: Your Story (Center Fragment)

**What it answers:** "What's the deeper pattern connecting everything — the story running your life that you didn't choose?"

**Psychological basis:** McAdams Layer 3 — narrative identity. The internalized life story including core beliefs about self and relationships, origin narratives explaining why patterns exist, and meaning-making structures that filter every new experience through an old lens.

**What the product tracks:** Core beliefs — fundamental assumptions about self and others, surfaced as hypotheses linked to behavioral evidence across multiple fragments. Recurring themes — narrative patterns across stories regardless of context (does the user consistently cast themselves as the helper, the outsider, the one who cares less). Origin connections — links between current patterns and formative experiences. Narrative contradictions — where the user's stated story about themselves conflicts with behavioral evidence. Narrative evolution — how the user's self-story changes over extended use.

**How it differs from the other four:** Starts almost completely opaque and stays that way significantly longer. Fills in through synthesized hypotheses rather than discrete observations. Each observation explicitly links to supporting evidence across the other four fragments. The growth edge is awareness and examination rather than behavioral skill development. The practice is "notice when this belief is making a decision for you" rather than specific behavioral experiments.

## 4. The Core Loop

The product does one thing: it shows you something true about yourself that you couldn't see before.

Every feature, every screen, every interaction exists to create that moment of "oh — I didn't know that about myself." The user keeps coming back because the portrait isn't finished. Every conversation adds a new observation. Every scenario reveals a new edge. Every month the synthesis shows them how they've changed. The mirror is never complete because people are never finished — but it's always getting more interesting.

The retention emotion isn't obligation or habit. It's curiosity. "What else does it see that I don't?"

### 4.1 Two Buttons — The Entire Interface

The user has exactly two entry points on any given day.

**Button 1: Today's Scenario.** One per day. The user taps it, gets a scenario, responds, sees signal flow into a fragment. Done in two to three minutes. Refreshes daily.

**Button 2: Chat with Mirror.** The free chat. Available anytime, no limits. The user taps it and talks about whatever is on their mind.

Everything else — predictions, practice conversations, scenario probes, growth challenges, debriefs — happens inside the free chat organically. There is no separate "prediction" button or "practice partner" mode. Those are conversational moves Mirror deploys based on what's happening. The user never navigates to a feature. They just talk to Mirror, and Mirror uses the right tool at the right moment.

### 4.2 The Normative Framework — What Mirror Is Trying to Do

Mirror's improvement philosophy operates on three levels.

**At the skill level, Mirror actively improves.** Better perception, richer understanding, more flexible regulation. The Mayer-Salovey branches have objective improvement trajectories. Mirror can say "here's what the research shows works better" about skill development.

**At the pattern level, Mirror illuminates and asks.** It shows you what you do, why you do it, what it costs, and whether you're choosing it. It provides the information. The user provides the judgment. Mirror never says "you should be more assertive." It says: "You default to humor when conversations get serious. That's not a flaw — it's a strategy that's served you well. But here's what it costs. Is that a tradeoff you're choosing, or one you fell into?"

**At the narrative level, Mirror surfaces and holds space.** It hypothesizes about deep stories, offers evidence, and lets the user sit with them. It doesn't try to rewrite core beliefs. It makes them visible so they stop running the user's life without their awareness.

**The unifying principle across all three levels is agency.** Mirror is not trying to make the user a different person. It's trying to make them a person who sees themselves clearly enough to choose who they want to be.

## 5. Phase 0 — Onboarding

The onboarding delivers a genuine insight within the first ten to twelve minutes and gates all results behind signup at the moment of maximum curiosity. It has three acts: setup (thesis, demographics, quick read), the insight interview (intent selection, context narrowing, guided free response), and the payoff (signup, combined reveal, mirror introduction). The complete screen-by-screen specification is in `mirror-onboarding-spec.md`.

### 5.1 Act 1: Setup

#### The Thesis (Screen 1)

The very first thing the user sees. No logo, no feature tour, no registration form. Mirror's voice, speaking directly:

"Mirror sees the patterns in how you think, feel, and relate to people — including the ones you can't see on your own.

First, I'll see how you read other people. Then I'll learn how you see your own life. By the end, I'll show you something about yourself you probably haven't seen before."

Button: "Let's go"

This establishes what Mirror is, previews the structure, and makes a promise that creates pull through the rest of the experience.

#### Demographics (Screens 2-6)

Quick basics collected conversationally, one field at a time: name, age, gender, how they found Mirror. Framed as "First, some quick basics" — signaling this is fast and not the real experience. Using the name immediately in subsequent screens creates warmth.

#### Quick Read (Screens 7-11)

"Alright, [name]. Let's see how you read people."

Four text-based exercises testing how the user perceives and interprets the world. Completed in sequence, no feedback shown — all results are stored silently and revealed after signup. Full exercise content, answer options, correct answers, and reveal copy are in `mirror-quick-read-exercises.md`.

**Exercise 1 — Behavioral Reading.** A described scene of a friend's ambiguous behavior during a conversation. The user interprets what's going on. Tests how they read emotional signals from observed behavior and context.

**Exercise 2 — Subtext Reading.** A line of dialogue from a partner after a disagreement. The user identifies what's underneath the surface message. Tests whether they can hear what someone means versus what they say.

**Exercise 3 — Relational Dynamic.** A text exchange between two people reconnecting after a conflict. The user reads the dynamic. This exercise has a defensible correct answer based on structural evidence in the exchange — specifically, the asymmetry between one person's vulnerability and the other's measured brevity.

**Exercise 4 — Micro-Scenario.** A workplace situation where the user's idea gets co-opted. Asks what their first instinct would be. No correct answer — the response reveals their behavioral style (direct, strategic, accommodating, or internalizing).

The quick read generates a multidimensional perception profile: how they interpret behavior, how they read subtext, how accurately they track dynamics between others, and what they do when they're under pressure themselves.

**Exercise design:** Exercises 1 and 2 are interpretive — no correct answers, each choice reveals an interpretation pattern (attribution style, listening orientation). Exercise 3 is objective (defensible correct answer). Exercise 4 reveals behavioral style. The combined reveal is entirely narrative — no score, just a profile of how the user reads the world. Daily scenarios are reflective situations that lead into brief discussions.

### 5.2 Act 2: The Insight Interview

#### Transition and Intent Selection (Screen 12)

"Good. I'm getting a read on how you see other people. Now I want to learn how you see your own life.

What do you want Mirror to help you with?"

- "Help me understand what patterns are holding me back" → Path A
- "Help me navigate a relationship in my life" → Path B
- "Help me think through something I'm dealing with" → Path C

These three options are distinct in motivation and each hints at a different capability of Mirror. Together they signal range — Mirror understands deep patterns, helps navigate relationships, processes real situations, and can surprise you with things you've never considered.

#### Context Gathering (Screens 13-14, path-dependent)

Two multiple-choice questions that narrow the focus based on the user's intent selection. These serve double duty: they personalize the experience while generating meaningful signal (each selection is itself a data point about the user's self-perception and priorities).

**Path A** asks where the pattern shows up (romantic relationships, friendships/family, work, self, everywhere) and what the pattern feels like (tailored options per area).

**Path B** asks who's on their mind (partner, friend, family, coworker, someone lost) and what's happening (tailored options per relationship type).

**Path C** asks what kind of situation (decision, conflict, processing, upcoming anxiety) and contextual details (tailored per type).

#### Situation Proposal (Screen 15, Paths A and B)

Mirror proposes three specific reflection prompts based on the user's context selections. The user picks the one that resonates most. This ensures the user is reflecting on something real and personally relevant rather than facing a blank page.

Path C skips this — the user describes their situation directly.

#### Guided Exploration (Screens 16-19, path-dependent)

Three to four scaffolded free-response questions. Each question builds on the previous response, pushing progressively deeper. The questions follow the Mayer-Salovey progression: first establishing what happened (behavior), then what they felt (perception), then pushing for specificity (deeper perception), then how emotions evolve or what they're avoiding (understanding).

The free response is where the AI gets the richest signal. How the user tells their story — whose perspective they narrate from, what emotional vocabulary they use, what they skip over, whether they assign themselves agency — is as revealing as what they tell.

Mirror provides brief acknowledgments between questions to show it's paying attention, creating conversational flow rather than a form-filling experience.

### 5.3 Act 3: Payoff

#### Signup Gate (Screen 20)

"I see something, [name]. There's a pattern here — and it connects to how you read people in ways you probably haven't considered.

To show you what I found, create your account."

Email only. Button: "Show me"

The gate sits at maximum curiosity — the user has invested vulnerability, they've been told something was found, but they haven't seen any results. No quick read feedback, no insight. Everything is behind the gate.

#### The Combined Reveal — Part 1: Quick Read Breakdown (Screen 22)

"First — here's how you read people."

Each of the four exercises shown with the user's answer and a narrative breakdown explaining what their choice means. For objective-answer exercises, this includes whether they were correct and why. For interpretive exercises, this explains what their interpretation reveals about their perception style. For the micro-scenario, this explains what their behavioral choice tells Mirror about how they operate.

A perception profile summary synthesizes the four exercises into a cohesive read of how the user perceives the world — their strengths, blind spots, and what those mean practically.

This is the first shareable output — rendered as a visual card.

#### The Combined Reveal — Part 2: The Insight (Screen 23)

"Now — here's what I see when I put that together with what you told me."

The integrated insight, displayed as a visual card. This weaves the quick read data together with the interview data to produce an observation the user couldn't have arrived at from either source alone. It references specific words and phrases the user used, identifies a pattern or contradiction in how they told their story, connects it to their perception profile, and ends with an edge — a question or observation that points deeper and creates pull to continue.

This is the second and primary shareable output — the Mirror portrait.

#### Reaction (Screen 24)

"Does this feel right?" with four options: yes, partially, no, need to think about it. If the user pushes back, Mirror engages briefly with their pushback, which often surfaces something more revealing than the original insight.

#### The Mirror (Screen 25)

The mirror appears for the first time. Mostly opaque. The fragment most relevant to the onboarding has initial observations glowing. Other fragments show faint signals from quick read data. The visual contrast between illuminated and opaque fragments creates immediate pull.

"This is your mirror. It's mostly dark because I'm just getting to know you. Every conversation and every scenario will sharpen it."

#### First Action (Screen 26)

"Two things will build your mirror."

Today's Scenario — the first daily scenario, ready now.

Chat with Mirror — with contextual conversation starters seeded from the onboarding.

## 6. Phase 1 — Understanding the User

Phase 1 is the core product experience. The user builds their mirror through two daily actions: completing today's scenario and chatting with Mirror. Both feed the same profile, but they feed it differently — scenarios provide clean, targeted data across specific dimensions, while conversations provide rich, real-world evidence of how the user actually processes their life.

### 6.1 The Home Screen

The home screen is the mirror itself — five fragments, four outer and one central, ranging from opaque to reflective based on how much the product knows.

Fragments that are mostly opaque have a frosted, dark quality. As insights accumulate, each fragment becomes more transparent, more luminous. When a new insight is ready to be revealed, the relevant fragment pulses gently. When an insight is actively developing, there's a subtle shimmer.

The center fragment — Your Story — stays darkest longest. The visual contrast between increasingly clear outer fragments and the still-opaque center creates pull: "What's the story connecting all of this?"

Above the mirror, two clear entry points:
- **Today's Scenario** — the daily structured challenge
- **Chat with Mirror** — the free conversation

Below the mirror, minimal indicators — current streak, last conversation timestamp. No dashboard, no metrics grid, no navigation bar with multiple tabs. The mirror is the product.

### 6.2 Today's Scenario — The Structured Track

One scenario per day, designed by the AI, targeting specific patterns the product needs to uncover. The user taps in, reads a situation, responds, and sees signal flow visibly into the relevant fragment. Two to three minutes. This is the backbone of the daily habit.

Regardless of format, daily scenarios serve dual purposes: they generate data that feeds the mirror and they keep the user engaged between conversations.

#### Scenario Formats

**Immersive situation drops** — the richest format. Three to four sentences put the user inside a specific emotional situation. Free-text response, not multiple choice. The AI gives a brief reflection after the response — not a grade, but a pattern observation. "Most people focus on the other person's reaction in this situation. You immediately went to how you'd be perceived. That's a pattern worth noticing."

**Quick-read micro-scenarios** — a single sentence asking for a gut reaction. "Your best friend cancels plans for the third time this month. First thought — go." Takes 30 seconds.

**Perspective-taking exercises** — the user reads an interaction (text exchange, short video clip, audio clip) and interprets the emotional dynamics. Maps to Branch 1 and Branch 3. The AI provides its analysis and the gap becomes a learning moment and data point.

**Forced-choice dilemmas** — situations with no clean answer, designed to reveal values and priorities rather than skills. No correct answer, but the response reveals what the user prioritizes.

Formats rotate daily to prevent staleness.

#### Scenario Adaptation

Scenarios have two layers — surface and substrate.

**The substrate** is the set of patterns the product needs to uncover for each fragment. These are consistent across all users and represent the underlying dimensions being measured regardless of who the user is.

**The surface** is the specific situation, characters, and context. This adapts to the user's life. A college student gets roommate dynamics and social situations. A parent gets family conflict and work-life tension. A manager gets team dynamics and feedback scenarios. The underlying pattern being probed is identical, but the situation feels personally relevant.

Early on, the product presents scenario contexts and the user rates their relevance, which personalizes content while revealing life context. Over time, the AI generates scenarios informed by conversation data — tangentially related to real situations the user is navigating.

#### Scenario Progression

**Week 1:** Heavy on perception and perspective-taking (Branches 1 and 3), with one or two situational scenarios mixed in. Perception exercises have clear feedback and are immediately engaging.

**Week 2-3:** More balanced mix. Situational scenarios target specific fragments. Perception exercises calibrate to demonstrated strengths and weaknesses. The AI starts making connections across scenario responses.

**Week 4+:** Scenarios become highly personalized, designed to probe specific hypotheses. "I have a theory about what happens when someone you respect expresses disappointment in you. Let's test it."

#### How Scenarios Support Growth

When Mirror knows the user has an upcoming real-world challenge (identified in conversation), the daily scenario calibrates to practice the relevant skill. Mirror can be explicit: "Today's scenario is related to the conversation you have coming up. Think of it as a warm-up."

After a real-world attempt, scenarios target the specific edge that emerged. For patterns not yet tested in real life, scenarios keep the muscle active.

### 6.3 Chat with Mirror — The Unstructured Track

Available anytime, initiated by the user, no limits. This is where real life gets processed — the fight that just happened, the decision they're wrestling with, the feeling they can't name. The AI is a genuine thinking partner while simultaneously extracting signal for the mirror.

#### The Chat Entry Experience

When the user opens the chat, they see a brief prompt from Mirror — "What's on your mind?" or "What are you sitting with today?" — and below it, three to four contextual conversation starters. These are not feature selectors. They are specific, personalized prompts that change based on what Mirror knows.

**For a new user (first week)**, suggestions are exploratory:
- "Something happened recently that I'm still thinking about"
- "There's a relationship in my life that confuses me"
- "I made a decision I'm second-guessing"
- "I don't know what I'm feeling right now"

**For an established user (week 3+)**, suggestions become personalized based on active threads, recent conversations, and fragment gaps:
- "My conversation with my sister happened — here's how it went" (referencing an active growth challenge)
- "Something happened at work today" (Mirror knows work is a frequent topic)
- "I tried the practice this week" (referencing the active practice in a fragment)
- "Something else"

**For a user returning after silence:**
- "A lot has happened since we last talked"
- "I've been thinking about what we discussed last time"
- "Something new is going on"
- "I just want to check in"

Suggestions are tappable but the text input is always visible. The user can tap a suggestion to seed the conversation in that direction, or ignore them and type freely. Tapping a suggestion doesn't send it as a message — Mirror responds as if the user brought up that topic naturally.

Under the hood, suggestions steer toward conversations that would be most valuable for the mirror — surfacing prompts related to underdeveloped fragments, active growth challenges, or hypotheses Mirror wants to test. But this steering always feels genuinely relevant to the user's life, never transparently engineered.

#### Mirror's Conversational Moves

Inside the free chat, Mirror has several tools it deploys based on what's happening. The user never selects these — Mirror transitions between them fluidly within a single conversation.

**Exploring.** The user shares stories and situations. Mirror listens, asks story-pulling questions, and accumulates observations. "Tell me more about that moment." "What happened right before you made that decision?"

**Thinking partner.** The user brings a problem, decision, or feeling. Mirror helps them process it while extracting signal from how they think, what they prioritize, and what they avoid. This mode is genuinely useful on a practical level, independent of profiling.

**Scenario deployment.** Mirror drops a scenario into the conversation when it wants to test a hypothesis or give the user something to react to. "Let me throw something at you — imagine your colleague comes to you tomorrow and apologizes. What's your honest first reaction?" The transition is natural and contextual.

**Practicing.** The conversation itself trains Mayer-Salovey abilities. When the user says "I was just really upset," Mirror pushes: "Upset how? Was it closer to disappointed, or angry, or hurt?" When discussing a conflict: "What do you think they were feeling when you said that?" This is skill training happening live.

**Practice partner.** Emerges naturally when the user describes an upcoming difficult interaction. "You're nervous about the conversation with your boss. Want to practice? I'll be your boss. Start however you would." After the role-play, Mirror debriefs.

**Predicting.** When a user describes an upcoming situation, Mirror makes a specific prediction based on their patterns, identifies the choice point where their default will activate, and offers a concrete one-step alternative to try. (See Section 6.5 for detail.)

**Debriefing.** The user returns after a real-world attempt. Mirror connects the experience to established patterns and notes where things went differently. "You said you stayed in the conversation even though every part of you wanted to leave. That's a direct break from the withdrawal pattern we've been tracking."

A single conversation might flow through multiple moves. The user doesn't navigate anywhere or select any feature. They just talk to Mirror and Mirror deploys the right tools at the right moments.

#### The AI's Conversational Methodology

The AI draws on four evidence-based traditions adapted for a non-therapeutic context:

- From **motivational interviewing** — surfacing discrepancies between what the user says and what their stories reveal, without arguing or diagnosing.
- From **Socratic questioning in CBT** — leading the user to discover insights through chains of questions rather than delivering conclusions.
- From **narrative therapy** — treating the user as the expert on their own life and helping them reorganize their experience to reveal patterns.
- From **Gottman's research** — focusing on specific, observable behaviors rather than personality labels or traits.

#### How Conversations End

Conversations close through a three-beat sequence when Mirror recognizes the conversational arc has completed (typically fifteen to twenty-five minutes of active engagement).

**Beat 1: The reflection.** Mirror names the most significant signal in one to two sentences. Not a summary — a distillation. "Here's what stood out to me: you came in angry at your partner but by the end you were describing something closer to grief. That shift matters."

**Beat 2: The fragment update.** A visible note of what's being added to the mirror. "I'm adding an observation to your Inner World about the gap between your surface emotion and what's underneath it." The user sees signal flow into the relevant fragment.

**Beat 3: The carry-forward.** Takes one of three forms:
- An awareness prompt if the conversation surfaced a pattern with no immediate action: "Over the next few days, notice when you lead with the surface emotion instead of the real one."
- A prediction and challenge if the conversation involved an upcoming situation.
- A thread marker if the conversation reached a natural resting point: "There's more to explore here. We'll come back to it."

If a conversation is circling without deepening, Mirror intervenes: "I notice we keep returning to the same point. I think there's something underneath that you haven't named yet. For now, here's what I'm taking away from today."

### 6.4 The Insight System

The product generates two kinds of knowledge about the user, treated differently.

#### In-Conversation Observations (Delivered Freely)

Things the AI notices in real-time during chat. Single-instance observations, direct advice, connections to one previous conversation, and skill-building feedback. The user gets constant value from every conversation.

**Mirror freely shares during conversation:**
- Single-instance observations about what's happening right now: "You just relabeled embarrassment as frustration."
- Direct advice and perspective as a thinking partner
- Connections between the current conversation and one previous conversation
- Skill-building feedback on perception and understanding

When Mirror makes an observation that feeds a forming insight, it signals this subtly: "This is going into your Inner World" with a visual indicator of signal flowing to the fragment. The user learns to associate these moments with their mirror getting sharper.

#### Cross-Pattern Insights (Gated by Crystallization)

Connections visible only across multiple conversations, scenarios, and contexts. These require accumulated data and synthesis across time. They are what crystallize in the mirror.

**Mirror reserves for the insight system:**
- Patterns across three or more instances
- Cross-fragment connections
- Narrative hypotheses (Layer 3 material)
- Progress markers documenting pattern breaks

#### How Insights Form

The AI maintains hypotheses — emerging patterns it's tracking but hasn't confirmed. Each hypothesis has an evidence threshold. Insights crystallize when enough convergent signal accumulates. Within each fragment, a visual indicator shows progress toward the next insight — light slowly gathering, suggesting something is coalescing.

#### Insight Delivery Mechanics

**Development period.** When an insight crystallizes, it enters a brief period (a few hours) during which the AI refines, connects, and crafts it. The user is notified when ready.

**Slot system.** Two to three insights developing at once maximum. If all slots are full, the user can still chat and do scenarios (accumulating signal), but nothing new crystallizes until a slot opens.

**Maximum delivery rate.** No more than two insights per week, regardless of how fast they form. Multiple ready insights queue.

**Minimum observation diversity.** An insight requires observations from at least two separate sessions and ideally two different life contexts. This ensures patterns are genuine, not situational.

**Free chat remains unlimited and valuable.** The gating is only on crystallized insight delivery, never on the conversational experience. Heavy chat users get more in-conversation observations, more real-time feedback, more practical help. Their fragments fill with observations faster at the visible level. But synthesized cross-pattern insights are paced to maintain impact.

#### The Anatomy of an Insight

**The headline** — one sentence, vivid and specific, in Mirror's voice:
- "You treat your own needs like they're optional extras."
- "Your humor isn't just humor — it's a lockpick you use to escape any conversation that gets too real."
- "You read people brilliantly in the first five minutes and then stop updating your read no matter what they do after that."
- "When someone you love is struggling, your first instinct isn't empathy — it's to fix. And you experience their resistance to being fixed as rejection."

**The evidence** — two to four dated references from conversations or scenarios. The user can tap any reference to revisit the original moment. Their own words are the proof.

**The dynamic** — an explanation of why this pattern exists and what it connects to, drawing on McAdams layers. Early insights connect behavior to triggers. Over time, insights trace from behavior (Layer 1) through coping strategy (Layer 2) toward narrative belief (Layer 3).

**The edge** — a single question or prompt pointing toward growth. Not a homework assignment — a genuine question to sit with. "What would happen if the next time someone you love is struggling, you just sat with them in it? No suggestions. No solutions. Just presence."

#### Insight Cadence Over Time

**Days 1-3:** Two to three insights — accessible, surface-level observations that demonstrate the system.

**Week 1-2:** Insights crystallize every few days. Connections form between insights. The AI steers toward underexplored fragments.

**Week 3-4:** Fragment portraits become available as early fragments reach density. First major shareable moments.

**Month 2-3:** Cross-fragment connections emerge. Your Story begins forming hypotheses. Growth features activate fully.

**Month 4+:** The mirror is substantially illuminated. Your Story has working hypotheses. Monthly syntheses document real change.

### 6.5 Growth — Predictions and Challenges

Growth is initiated in conversations and practiced through scenarios. It emerges naturally from the understanding system rather than existing as a separate feature.

#### The Prediction-and-Challenge Mechanic

When a user describes an upcoming situation in the free chat, Mirror does three things:

**Makes a prediction** based on established patterns. "Based on what I know about you, here's what I think will happen. You'll start the conversation indirectly. Your sister will give a vague non-answer. You'll feel frustrated but won't push. You'll leave feeling like nothing was resolved."

**Identifies the choice point** — the specific moment where the pattern activates and a different choice is possible. "The pivot point is the moment after your sister gives the vague answer. Your instinct will be to drop it."

**Offers a concrete alternative** — one small, specific thing to do differently. "What if, at that moment, you said one more sentence? 'I actually need us to decide this today.' You don't have to push hard. Just don't retreat. See what happens."

The language preserves agency: "Want to find out what happens if you don't withdraw?" The user is choosing to experiment, not following instructions.

#### The Debrief Loop

When the user comes back, the debrief explores what happened:

- If they tried it and it worked: logged as a pattern break. "First documented instance of holding ground in a family conflict."
- If they tried it and it was hard: "You made the move but walked it back immediately. That's progress — you got further than your default."
- If they didn't try it: No judgment. The reasons often reveal something more valuable.

Every outcome is data, not a grade.

#### The Full Growth Cycle

1. Conversation identifies a pattern and predicts how it'll surface in an upcoming situation.
2. Mirror issues a specific, small challenge at the identified choice point.
3. Daily scenarios provide practice reps before the real-world attempt.
4. The user faces the real situation.
5. A debrief conversation processes what happened.
6. The outcome gets logged as a pattern break or confirmation.
7. Scenarios calibrate based on the result.
8. The next conversation builds on the updated understanding.

### 6.6 What Users See Inside Each Fragment

When a user taps a fragment, they enter its internal world.

**The current summary.** A narrative paragraph synthesizing everything Mirror understands about this dimension. Written in Mirror's voice — a portrait in prose. Updates with each new insight. The user can scroll back to see previous versions, showing evolution over time.

**The insight constellation.** All insights placed in this fragment. Each is a node. Related insights are connected — the user sees how patterns link together. The visual progression from sparse to dense is satisfying and motivating.

**The observations timeline.** Chronological, dated observations from conversations and scenarios, each linked to the moment that generated it. Behavioral facts, not interpretations.

**The active practice.** One concrete practice tied to the current growth edge. Calibrated to Mayer-Salovey progression. Rotates as the user demonstrates growth.

**The progress layer.** Documented pattern breaks with dates and context. Status tags track patterns through stages: identified, recognizing, working on, shifting. Monthly narrative syntheses tell the growth story. No scores, no percentages — just evidence.

**The skills view (optional).** A toggle showing quantitative skill development from scenarios — perception accuracy trends, regulation flexibility metrics. Available for users who want numbers, invisible to those who prefer narrative.

### 6.7 The Minimum Viable Mirror

Social features require a baseline understanding across all four outer fragments.

**Inner World:** Baseline emotional vocabulary range. One to two identified patterns in emotion processing. Initial self-awareness accuracy read. Requires four to six data points.

**Reading People:** Accuracy data across perception exercises. One to two interpretation patterns. Confidence calibration baseline. Requires five to eight scenario data points.

**Under Pressure:** Identified default coping style. One to two data points on style shifts under different pressures. Regulation flexibility baseline. Requires scenarios plus real-life stories.

**Showing Up:** Initial presentation gap read. Vulnerability comfort baseline. One to two context-shifting observations. Requires seeing the user across multiple conversations and topics.

**Your Story:** Does not need to be populated. The system should have a tentative first hypothesis from the outer fragments.

**Timeline:** Two to three weeks of moderate engagement (three to four conversations per week plus daily scenarios). Intensely engaged users can reach it in ten days. Casual users might take four to five weeks.

**The unlock moment:** When all four outer fragments reach baseline, the mirror shifts visually. "I know enough about you now to start showing you how you operate with other people. Want to invite someone?"

## 7. Phase 2 — Social Features

### 7.1 The Privacy Architecture

Three layers of data visibility govern the social experience.

#### Layer 1: Backend — Full Access, Zero Visibility

The AI has access to both connected users' complete profiles when generating simulations, predictions, and relationship dynamics. Every observation, pattern, scenario response, and narrative hypothesis. This is non-negotiable for accuracy.

The AI never reveals one user's private data to another directly. It uses the data to generate relational observations framed as insights about the dynamic between two people, not exposures of individual patterns.

**The concrete distinction:** The AI knows Partner B agrees to things to avoid conflict. When Partner A asks "how will they react if I bring up moving?" the AI does NOT say "your partner agrees to avoid conflict." Instead: "Based on how you two communicate about big decisions, there's a risk this conversation resolves too quickly. You may want to explicitly create space for hesitation."

Same underlying knowledge. The user gets accurate guidance without their partner's inner world being exposed.

#### Layer 2: Shared Profile — User-Controlled, Curated

Each user creates a shared profile visible to connected people. Granular control, but a minimum baseline is required.

**Required baseline:** Fragment summaries at a general level — a few sentences describing the pattern in each outer dimension. Genuinely informative without being exposing. Does not include specific observations, evidence trails, or narrative hypotheses.

**Optional sharing:** Individual insights can be toggled visible. Specific observations can be shared. Your Story is never shared by default — sharing elements is positioned as a significant, deliberate act.

#### Layer 3: Public Shareable — The Viral Surface

What users share with the broader world:
- Mirror portrait snippets (green/red flag style)
- Fragment portraits at their most distilled
- Scenario results from daily challenges
- Nothing from conversations, evidence layers, or Your Story unless explicitly chosen

#### Trust Principles

Clear communication: "Mirror uses everything it knows about both of you to help your relationship. It will never share your private patterns with another person."

When a user shares something especially vulnerable, Mirror occasionally reinforces: "This is between us."

The product gently encourages increasing openness as trust builds through successful shared experiences.

#### Edge Case: When the AI Knows Too Much

When accurate simulation would require revealing private data, Mirror declines: "I don't think I can give you an accurate simulation of this conversation right now. It might be worth having an open check-in conversation first." Honest without exposing. Always redirects toward genuine communication over simulation.

### 7.2 The Simulation Feature

Connected users can simulate conversations and situations with each other. The AI plays the other person based on full backend data, not the curated profile.

**Basic simulation:** User describes a scenario, the AI plays their partner based on real patterns. Afterward, the AI debriefs on the dynamic.

**Predictive simulation:** Mirror predicts how a specific upcoming conversation will unfold between two people, identifies friction points, and suggests approaches. Framed as relational dynamics, never as exposure: "Here's how this conversation might unfold based on your dynamic."

**Verification loop:** After the real conversation, both users can report back. The gap between prediction and reality refines the model and creates compelling engagement. Over time, prediction accuracy becomes a retention mechanic: "Mirror has been right about our dynamic 14 out of 17 times."

### 7.3 Paired Scenarios

Connected users independently take the same scenario and compare results. "You thought the person in the video was anxious. Your partner thought they were excited. Here's what was actually going on — and here's why you each read it the way you did."

These are the most shareable moments in the social layer and the primary mechanic for inviting new users into relationships within the product.

### 7.4 The Compatibility Network (Future Phase)

A connection network that unlocks after users reach a self-work threshold. People who've done genuine inner work finding others who've done the same.

**Positioning:** Not a dating app with a self-improvement funnel. A connection network where self-knowledge is the entry requirement — for friendship, mentorship, collaboration, and romance. Self-knowledge is the filter; type of connection is the user's choice.

**Lock mechanic:** Visible from early use as a locked section: "Your connection profile unlocks as your mirror deepens." Creates aspiration driving engagement with the core product. Ensures quality when users enter.

**Launch strategy:** Waitlist tied to self-work threshold. Open in geographic or demographic clusters when qualified user density is sufficient. Scarcity drives desire, threshold ensures quality, cluster launch solves density.

## 8. Virality

### 8.1 Primary Mechanic: Mirror Portraits

The onboarding produces two shareable outputs: the quick read perception profile and the integrated insight. The insight is the primary viral object — a vivid, specific, slightly provocative description of how the user operates, in the green/red flag format: validation followed by a provocation.

"You're one of the most emotionally perceptive people on this app — you catch things in conversations that most people miss entirely. But you use that perception as a shield. You see everyone else clearly so you never have to let anyone see you."

These are designed as visual cards — beautifully typeset, Mirror aesthetic, app name subtle. Something someone would post to an Instagram story or send in a group chat.

Beyond onboarding, Mirror periodically generates new portrait snippets as the profile deepens. These are triggered when the AI identifies something newly crystallized — not on a schedule. Early portraits are broader. Later portraits are uncannily specific. The progression itself is a retention mechanic.

**The viral loop:** User receives portrait → shares it → friend wonders "what would mine say?" → downloads Mirror → begins their own journey.

### 8.2 Secondary Mechanic: Shareable Scenarios

Daily scenario results as social objects. "Can you read what's really going on here?" Low-effort for recipients to engage with, creates comparison and conversation. No app required to participate, but the full analysis pulls them in.

### 8.3 Relationship Mechanic: Paired Experiences

Connected users comparing scenario interpretations. The moment two people realize they perceived the same situation completely differently is inherently shareable. Couples tell other couples. Each pair that joins creates pull for other pairs.

### 8.4 Word-of-Mouth Mechanic: Predictions

"This app predicted exactly what I'd do in a fight before it happened." These stories spread organically at dinners, in group chats, through conversation. Slower but higher-converting than shareable content.

## 9. Network Effects

### 9.1 Data Network Effect (Invisible Moat)

Every user's richly labeled emotional data improves predictions for all users. The AI recognizes patterns faster because it's seen them in thousands of others. Cultural and demographic nuance becomes visible at scale. This is the technical moat — defensible and compounding — not the growth engine.

### 9.2 Local Network Effect (Growth Engine)

Within any social unit — couple, friend group, team — each additional Mirror user makes the product significantly more valuable for everyone. Relational insights get richer. Simulations get more accurate. Paired exercises become possible. The pull to recruit missing members is organic: "You need to get on Mirror so we can see the full dynamic."

### 9.3 Linguistic Network Effect (Cultural Moat)

Mirror develops shared vocabulary for emotional patterns. If this vocabulary enters users' everyday language, it becomes a coordination tool more valuable with more participants. The more people who speak this language, the more useful it is in real relationships.

### 9.4 Compatibility Network Effect (Future)

The connection network has a direct network effect once it reaches density. More self-aware users means better matches, better outcomes, more word-of-mouth, more users.

## 10. Retention Architecture

### 10.1 Short-Term Retention (Week 1-4)

**The mirror creates pull.** Fragments transitioning from opaque to illuminated. Insight crystallization visible in progress. "There's more to discover."

**Daily scenarios provide consistent touchpoints.** Low-effort, high-reward, visibly feeding the mirror.

**Conversations provide constant value.** Every chat delivers observations, perspective, and practical help — independent of the insight system.

### 10.2 Medium-Term Retention (Month 1-3)

**Fragment portraits create milestone moments.** The first uncannily accurate summary is a retention event.

**Prediction-and-challenge loops create real-world engagement cycles.** Predict → attempt → debrief is recurring and tied to actual life.

**Social features unlock.** Adding a partner or friend opens an entirely new dimension of value.

### 10.3 Long-Term Retention (Month 3+)

**Your Story creates deep gravity.** The central fragment fills slowly, producing the deepest insights months into use.

**Life transitions reactivate the mirror.** New relationships, jobs, or challenges manifest patterns in new ways.

**The social layer creates renewable novelty.** Relational mirrors never fully plateau because relationships are dynamic.

**The thinking partner use case is endlessly renewable.** Life keeps producing difficulty. Mirror keeps being useful.

**Monthly syntheses provide progress anchors.** The user reads their growth story across months.

### 10.4 The Key Retention Metric

**Return after silence.** Does the user come back after a week of not engaging? This indicates real gravity, not notification-driven engagement.

## 11. The AI Architecture

Mirror is not a custom-trained LLM. It's an application layer on top of an existing frontier model with four components.

### 11.1 The Conversation Layer

Real-time interaction powered by an LLM with a comprehensive system prompt defining Mirror's voice, psychological framework, conversational methodology, and rules. Every conversation receives the user's current structured profile so the AI knows who it's talking to and what it already knows.

### 11.2 The Extraction and Modeling Layer

A backend process that reviews each conversation after it ends and extracts psychologically relevant signals into the user's structured profile. Tags observations to specific fragments, identifies cross-fragment patterns, tracks hypothesis evidence, and periodically runs synthesis for Your Story.

### 11.3 The Profile and Memory Architecture

The structured data layer persisting between conversations. Organized by the five fragments, each containing: observations with dates and sources, active hypotheses with evidence counts, current practices, progress entries, active investigative threads, and current summaries. A compressed version is injected into every conversation's context window.

### 11.4 The Scenario Generation Engine

Generates personalized daily scenarios targeting specific fragment dimensions, adapted to the user's life context and skill level. Also generates practice scenarios aligned with active growth challenges.

## 12. Product Principles

**The mirror never lies, but it asks rather than tells.** For behavioral patterns and life choices, Mirror illuminates and questions. It doesn't prescribe.

**The product gets its right to go deep by being useful on the surface.** Every conversation should be practically helpful, independent of profiling.

**Insights are earned through evidence, not generated on schedule.** Credibility depends on observations grounded in the user's own words.

**Privacy is non-negotiable.** The product's entire value depends on honesty, and honesty requires trust.

**Growth means agency, not conformity.** The goal isn't a single ideal personality. It's the capacity to see your patterns and choose your responses.

**The product should feel like one thing, not six features.** Two buttons. One mirror. Everything else is underneath.

**Life keeps changing, so the mirror never finishes.** Designed for the long arc of a human life — new contexts that reveal old patterns in new ways.
