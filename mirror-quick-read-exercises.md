# Mirror — Quick Read Exercises (A/B Test Versions)

This document contains two complete versions of the quick read exercise set for A/B testing during onboarding validation.

**Version A (Objective):** All four exercises have defensible correct answers. Exercises 1-3 test perception accuracy. Exercise 4 reveals behavioral style (no correct answer). The combined reveal includes a score element: "You caught 2 out of 3" alongside the narrative profile.

**Version B (Interpretive):** Exercises 1-2 have no correct answer — each choice reveals an interpretation pattern. Exercise 3 tests perception accuracy. Exercise 4 reveals behavioral style. The combined reveal is entirely narrative — no score, just a profile of how the user reads the world.

Both versions use the same Exercise 3 and Exercise 4. They differ only in Exercises 1 and 2.

**What we're testing:** Does the objective scoring make the experience more engaging and shareable (supporting a daily EQ skill-building loop)? Or does the interpretive approach generate better insight data and a more satisfying reveal? Or is there no meaningful difference in user reaction?

# VERSION A: OBJECTIVE SCORING

---

## Exercise 1A: Behavioral Reading (Objective)

**What it tests:** Can you accurately identify what someone is experiencing based on described behavioral cues?

### The Scenario

"You and a friend are catching up over coffee. They've been telling you in great detail about their weekend — where they went, who they saw, what they ate. You've mostly been listening, giving short responses. Near the end of the conversation, they pause and say: 'Anyway. How are you? You've been quiet the past few days.'"

**What's going on with your friend?**

- They're checking in because they genuinely care about you
- They're worried you're pulling away and this is their way of testing the connection
- They just like sharing their life and the question at the end is an afterthought
- They feel guilty about talking about themselves too much

**Correct answer:** They're worried you're pulling away and this is their way of testing the connection

**Why:** The key phrase is "the past few days" — this isn't an observation about today's coffee. They've been tracking your reduced engagement across multiple days and multiple interactions. The long, detailed sharing about their weekend was a bid for engagement — an attempt to draw you back in by offering their life. When that bid got only short responses (you've been mostly listening, giving short responses), they raised the thing they've actually been worried about.

The structure is the tell: they buried the real concern at the end, after a long stretch of safe content. This is how people raise vulnerable relational concerns — they bury them after something easy. "Anyway" is a deliberate transition from safe to vulnerable.

If this were just genuine care about your wellbeing without relational anxiety, the framing would be less specific — "is everything okay?" or "you seem off lately." "You've been quiet the past few days" specifically names your behavior toward them. Quiet with whom? With them. They're tracking the silence in the relationship.

If it were guilt about talking too much, they'd reference this conversation — "sorry, I've been going on." If it were an afterthought, "the past few days" wouldn't be there — they'd just say "how are you?"

**Reveal copy:**
- If correct: "You caught the buried signal. The long message about their weekend was the safe part — easy to share, easy to engage with. 'You've been quiet the past few days' is the real message, and they put it last because it's the most vulnerable thing in the conversation. They're reaching out while giving you cover to respond to the easy part if you don't want to address the real thing."
- If incorrect (genuinely checking in): "They are checking in — but 'you've been quiet the past few days' is doing more than that. It's an observation that reveals they've been tracking your engagement across days. People don't monitor your silence patterns unless the silence is affecting them. This isn't general concern — it's relational concern."
- If incorrect (afterthought): "If this were just a polite pivot, the sentence would be 'anyway, how are you?' Adding 'you've been quiet the past few days' transforms it. That phrase carries weight — it's a specific observation they've been sitting on. Afterthoughts don't include behavioral tracking across days."
- If incorrect (guilty): "Guilt about dominating the conversation would sound like 'sorry, I've been going on forever — your turn.' It would reference today, not the past few days. The time reference shifts this from 'I talked too much right now' to 'I've noticed something about you that worries me.'"

## Exercise 2A: Subtext Reading (Objective)

**What it tests:** Can you identify what someone means underneath what they say, when the surface message and the actual message diverge?

### The Scenario

"After a disagreement, your partner says: 'Look, I don't want to fight. I just want us to be okay.'"

**What are they really saying?**

- They're genuinely prioritizing the relationship over winning the argument
- They want the conflict to end without actually resolving what caused it
- They're apologizing indirectly without saying sorry
- They're exhausted and need a break from the conversation

**Correct answer:** They want the conflict to end without actually resolving what caused it

**Why:** "I don't want to fight" reframes the conversation as a fight rather than a disagreement — which positions continuing to discuss it as aggression. This is a subtle but powerful rhetorical move: by calling it a fight, they make any further engagement feel hostile. "I just want us to be okay" prioritizes the surface state (being okay) over the underlying issue. Together, these statements are a request to stop engaging with the content of the disagreement and return to equilibrium. This isn't resolution — it's premature closure.

Someone genuinely prioritizing the relationship would engage with what caused the tension, not ask to skip past it. Prioritizing a relationship means caring enough to work through the hard part, not asking to pretend it's over. Someone apologizing indirectly would take some responsibility — "I know I was harsh" or "I could have said that differently." This person takes no responsibility. Someone needing a break would ask for a break — "can we come back to this later?" — not for everything to be okay right now.

**Reveal copy:**
- If correct: "You read the request underneath the words. 'I don't want to fight' recategorizes the disagreement as a fight — which makes continuing it feel aggressive. 'I just want us to be okay' is asking for the surface to smooth over without the underneath being addressed. It's a bid for peace that skips past resolution."
- If incorrect (prioritizing the relationship): "The intention might be genuine but the mechanism is avoidance. Prioritizing a relationship means engaging with what's wrong, not asking to skip past it. 'I just want us to be okay' is asking for the feeling of resolution without the work of it."
- If incorrect (apologizing indirectly): "An indirect apology takes some responsibility — 'I know that was a lot' or 'I didn't mean to upset you.' This person isn't taking responsibility for anything. They're asking for the conflict to end without addressing what caused it or their role in it."
- If incorrect (exhausted, need a break): "Someone who needs a break asks for a break — 'can we talk about this tomorrow?' They're requesting a pause, not an ending. 'I just want us to be okay' isn't asking to pause. It's asking to be done. Those are very different requests."

# VERSION B: INTERPRETIVE

---

## Exercise 1B: Behavioral Reading (Interpretive)

**What it tests:** When you see ambiguous behavior, where does your mind go? Your interpretation reveals your default attribution style.

### The Scenario

"You're at a small gathering. A friend who's usually talkative and warm greets everyone when they arrive but then spends most of the evening on the edge of conversations — laughing at the right moments, responding when spoken to, but not initiating anything. At one point you catch them staring out the window for a few seconds before someone pulls them back in."

**What do you think is going on?**

- They're preoccupied with something unrelated to the gathering — work, a text they got, something on their mind
- They're feeling disconnected from this group lately and aren't sure they belong anymore
- They're tired or low-energy and doing their best to be present despite not being fully up for it
- They had a disagreement with someone at the gathering and they're managing the discomfort

**What each choice reveals (internal — not shown to user):**

- "Preoccupied with something external" → External-cause attribution. They read behavior as the product of outside circumstances. Observational, distanced read.
- "Feeling disconnected from the group" → Belonging-oriented read. Sensitive to social positioning and exclusion. Often correlates with the user's own experiences of belonging.
- "Tired and doing their best" → Generous/normalizing interpretation. Either genuine empathy or a pattern of minimizing signals.
- "Disagreement with someone here" → Interpersonal-tension read. Assumes the cause is someone specific in the environment. Sensitive to conflict undercurrents.

**Reveal framing by selection:**
- External cause: "Your instinct is to look for what happened before the room. You read behavior as the product of circumstances. That's an observational, distanced read — you're tracking carefully but you may miss when the cause is actually in the room."
- Disconnection: "You went straight to belonging. Someone pulled back and your mind went to 'they don't feel like they fit.' That sensitivity to social connection tells me something about what you track in groups."
- Tired/generous: "You gave the gentlest read. That might be accurate, or it might be a pattern of explaining away signals that deserve more attention. The question is whether your generous interpretations are usually right or whether they sometimes let you avoid seeing something uncomfortable."
- Interpersonal tension: "You assumed someone specific caused this. You read the behavior as relational and directed. That tells me you're tuned into interpersonal undercurrents — which means you're probably good at reading rooms but may also see conflict where there's something else going on."

## Exercise 2B: Subtext Reading (Interpretive)

**What it tests:** When someone says something with potential subtext, what do you hear? Your interpretation reveals your default listening orientation.

### The Scenario

"You've been dating someone for a few months. After a night out with your friends — which they didn't come to — they say: 'Sounds like you had a great time.'"

**What are they really saying?**

- They're genuinely glad you had fun — no subtext
- They feel left out and are flagging it indirectly
- They're testing whether you missed them or even noticed they weren't there
- They're fine but slightly jealous of the fun you had without them

**What each choice reveals (internal — not shown to user):**

- "Genuinely glad" → Trusts surface communication. Either doesn't read subtext naturally or actively chooses not to assume hidden meaning.
- "Feel left out" → Reads hurt underneath the statement. Hears a bid for inclusion disguised as a comment.
- "Testing" → Reads strategy and evaluation. Hears the statement as a probe gathering data about their importance.
- "Fine but slightly jealous" → Reads a mixed emotional state. Hears something nuanced — not hurt, not strategic, just a minor uncomfortable feeling expressed lightly.

**Reveal framing by selection:**
- Genuinely glad: "You hear words and trust them. That means you probably respond to what people say rather than what they might mean — which is straightforward, but it can leave subtext unaddressed."
- Feel left out: "You heard hurt underneath a neutral sentence. That makes you attentive to unspoken needs — and it might also mean you hear hurt in places where there isn't any."
- Testing: "You heard a strategic move — not just a feeling but a test. That's a surveillance-oriented read that might tell me something about how you experience being evaluated yourself."
- Fine but jealous: "You heard mixed feelings — not a crisis, not a test, just a complicated small reaction. You allow people to feel more than one thing at once, which is a nuanced read."

## Exercise 3: Relational Dynamic (Objective — Both Versions)

**What it tests:** Can you accurately read the dynamic between two people based on structural evidence in a text exchange?

### The Scenario

```
Person A: I've been thinking about what you said
Person B: Which part?
Person A: All of it honestly
Person B: Ok
Person A: I think you were right
Person B: I know. But thank you for saying that.
```

**What's happening between these two people?**

- Person A is apologizing and Person B is accepting gracefully
- Person B is asserting dominance — making Person A admit they were wrong
- Both are reconnecting, but Person B is still guarded
- Person A is trying to end the conflict and Person B isn't ready to move on

**Correct answer:** Both are reconnecting, but Person B is still guarded

**Why:** Person A is making a clear bid for reconnection — "I've been thinking about what you said" and "I think you were right" require swallowing pride. These are vulnerable messages. Person B's responses are short but engaged. "Which part?" is curiosity, not dismissal — a dismissive person would say "ok" or nothing. "I know. But thank you for saying that" is layered — the "but" separates "I was right" from "I appreciate you acknowledging it," showing they care about the relationship, not just about being right.

But the overall brevity from Person B — "which part?", "ok", short responses throughout — contrasted with Person A's vulnerability shows asymmetry. Person A is opening up. Person B is receiving but not fully opening back. That's guardedness. The hurt hasn't fully resolved. They're thawing, not thawed.

**Reveal copy:**
- If correct: "You read the movement underneath the brevity. Person A is reaching out. Person B's short responses look cold but they're engaged — 'which part?' is curiosity, and 'but thank you' shows they care about the relationship. What's missing is matching vulnerability. Person B is letting Person A do the work of reconnection. That's not coldness. That's guardedness."
- If incorrect (accepting gracefully): "Gracious acceptance is warm and open. Person B's responses throughout are measured and brief. 'Thank you for saying that' is appreciation, but the brevity everywhere else is distance."
- If incorrect (asserting dominance): "'But thank you for saying that' undermines the dominance read. Someone asserting power would leave it at 'I know.' The addition shows they care about the relationship, not just about being right."
- If incorrect (not ready to move on): "Person B is engaging. Someone not ready to move on would be colder or more resistant. This person is participating. They're just doing it carefully."

## Exercise 4: Micro-Scenario (Behavioral Style — Both Versions)

**What it tests:** When you're under social or emotional pressure, what's your instinctive response? No correct answer — each option maps to a behavioral style.

### The Scenario

"You're in a group meeting and share an idea. It gets a lukewarm response and the conversation moves on. A few minutes later, someone else brings up essentially the same idea in slightly different words and everyone responds enthusiastically. What's your first instinct?"

- Speak up and point out you said the same thing earlier (Direct)
- Say nothing in the meeting but bring it up with the person privately later (Strategic)
- Let it go — what matters is that the idea is moving forward (Accommodating)
- Say nothing and stew about it for the rest of the day (Internalizing)

*Style labels are internal only — never shown to the user.*

**Reveal by selection:**
- Direct: "Your instinct is to name what happened in the moment. You value fairness over social comfort and you're willing to accept the awkwardness of correcting the record. This tells me you have a low tolerance for feeling invisible."
- Strategic: "You want to address it but on your terms, in a controlled setting. You're managing the social calculus — getting recognition without creating a scene. That's sophisticated, but it also means your real reactions are always on a delay."
- Accommodating: "You default to letting it go. Whether that's genuine detachment from credit or a learned pattern of making yourself smaller to avoid friction — that's the question. Most people who truly don't care wouldn't have registered it happening."
- Internalizing: "You feel it but you don't act on it. The frustration has nowhere to go so it stays inside. This pattern — feeling strongly but expressing nothing — shows up somewhere in your life. Mirror will find where."

# HOW THE EXERCISES FEED THE INSIGHT

## Version A (Objective) — Data Generated

- Exercise 1: Correct/incorrect + which wrong answer they chose (reveals attribution bias even when wrong)
- Exercise 2: Correct/incorrect + which wrong answer they chose (reveals listening orientation even when wrong)
- Exercise 3: Correct/incorrect + which wrong answer they chose
- Exercise 4: Behavioral style selection

The AI has: perception accuracy (0-3 out of 3 objective questions), specific blind spots (which wrong answers were chosen), and behavioral instinct. The insight can reference accuracy directly: "You caught the relational anxiety underneath your friend's question but you missed the avoidance disguised as peacemaking — you took 'I just want us to be okay' at face value."

## Version B (Interpretive) — Data Generated

- Exercise 1: Attribution style (external-cause / belonging / generous / interpersonal-tension)
- Exercise 2: Listening orientation (trusts-surface / reads-hurt / reads-strategy / reads-mixed-feelings)
- Exercise 3: Correct/incorrect + which wrong answer they chose
- Exercise 4: Behavioral style selection

The AI has: interpretation patterns (what the user defaults to when reading ambiguity), one perception accuracy data point (exercise 3), and behavioral instinct. The insight can reference patterns: "When things are ambiguous, you assume the problem is about you — you read relational threat into both scenarios. But when you can see a clear dynamic between two other people, you're accurate. Your perception is sharp when you're not in the picture. It gets distorted when you are."

## What to Compare in Testing

For each test user, record:
1. Which version they received (A or B)
2. Time spent on each exercise (engagement proxy)
3. Whether they tried to discuss or debate their answers (interest proxy)
4. Their rating of the combined reveal (accuracy, novelty, share impulse — using the testing framework scales)
5. Unprompted comments about the quick read section specifically

**Key questions:**
- Do Version A users rate the combined reveal higher on shareability? (Tests the EQ score virality hypothesis)
- Do Version B users rate the combined reveal higher on accuracy and novelty? (Tests the richer-data hypothesis)
- Is there a measurable difference in engagement during the exercises themselves?
- Do users in either version spontaneously ask to compare with a friend?
